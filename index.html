Introduction

Hello! My name is Lily, and I am excited to tell you about what I've learned during my co-op experiences! First, I want to give myself a little introduction. I am a fourth year Cell and Molecular Biology student at Northeastern University, and I am originally from northern New Jersey. My favorite hobbies have been going to the gym, reading, cooking, and photography. First, I wanted to begin by how I became interested in biology. It started when I was in ninth grade and I was in my first ever biology class. I remember being mesmerized and fascinated by the molecular mechanisms that allow everyday life to thrive, from breathing, to plants growing, to how our body is able to develop from a fetus to an adult. It absolutely amazed me how such small pathways, such small molecules, could collectively make such a huge imapct on a person's life. One small mutation could lead to a genetic disease that has long-lasting effects; for example, sickle cell anemia is caused by one nucleotide mutation in the HBB gene, which then gets applied to the rest of the blood cells in the body to have a deformed and crescent shape, instead of a round and circular one.

Over my time at Northeastern, I have completed two co-ops, both of which were industry biotechnology co-ops focused on cancer therapeutics. My first co-op was at Lytica Therapeutics that specialized in a therapeutic called antibody-drug conjugates or (ADC). These were basically antibodies that were conjugated, or linked, to payloads that were typically cytotoxic drugs meant to kill cancer cells. My second co-op was at Tango Therapeutics, which also conducted protein science with cancer but developed a different type of therapeutic called small molecular inhibitors. These are more well-known therapeutics that are used to inhibit the active site of a particular target protein that is involved in tumorigenesis (usually an oncogenic protein). After my industry co-ops, I wanted to experience academic research, so currently I am in the Monaghan Lab that studies regeneration in axolotls. I have pictured one of the axolotls to the right :)
